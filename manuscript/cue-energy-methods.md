# Research Design

The purpose of the study is to examine how partisan or climate change cues could shift the general public's preferred energy mix.  I use a survey experiment to test several hypotheses regarding the impact of the cues on the shifts in the preferred energy mix across political beliefs. The preferred energy mix is the dependent variable, and is measured as the percentage each respondent allocated across the five energy sources, including fossil fuels, wind, solar, hydro, and nuclear. As part of the research design, respondents were first given the current energy mix of the US related to electricity, which is that the US obtains 58 percent of our electricity from fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas), 22 percent from renewable sources (wind, solar, and hydropower), 18 percent from nuclear energy, and 2 percent from other sources (wood, biofuels, waste products, and geothermal energy). Therefore, respondents were anchored to the actual energy mix use to produce electricity in the US, as of 2024 the most recent year where data was available. Following the information regarding the current US energy mix, respondents were randomly assigned to one of three experimental conditions, including a Trump cue, a climate cue, and a control condition with no cues. The preferred energy mix is a within-subjects factor, the cues are between-subjects experimental (manipulated) factors, and political beliefs - a combined measure of ideology and partisanship - is a between-subjects measured moderator. 

## Hypotheses

The research design is intended to test the following hypotheses, which were preregistered using the Open Science Framework (OSF)^[See here: https://osf.io/s2ku8/overview?view_only=8d073e8b77f44768a27184b9b0aff785]

> Conservative Republicans who receive the Trump cue will support a _higher_ percentage of fossil fuels than those in the climate change or control group, and those with other political beliefs

> Conservative Republicans who receive the climate cue will support a _higher_ percentage of fossil fuels than those in the Trump or control group, and those with other political beliefs

> Conservative Republicans who receive the Trump cue will support a _lower_ percentage of renewable energy sources than those in the climate change or control group, and those with other political beliefs

> Conservative Republicans who receive the climate cue will support a _lower_ percentage of renewable energy sources than those in the climate change or control group, and those with other political beliefs

> Liberal Democrats who receive the Trump cue will support a _lower_ percentage of fossil fuels than those in the climate change or control group, and those with other political beliefs 

> Liberal Democrats who receive the Trump cue will support a _higher_ percentage of renewable energy sources than those in the climate change or control group, and those with other political beliefs 

> Liberal Democrats who receive the climate cue will support a _lower_ percentage of fossil fuels than those in the climate change or control group, and those with other political beliefs 

> Liberal Democrats who receive the climate cue will support a _higher_ percentage of renewable energy sources than those in the climate change or control group, and those with other political beliefs 

## Data

The data for this study comes from a survey of US residents recruited through Cloud Research.[^See: https://www.cloudresearch.com]. The sample is quota-based using information from the US Census Bureau, and survey weights were applied to ensure that the sample was representative.^[For more information on the sample and the weighting procedures see the online methods section.] As noted, respondents were randomally assigned to one of three conditions, including a Trump cue, a climate change cue, or a control condition with no cues. The Trump cue was in the form of a question regarding agreement, on a 1 strongly disagree to 5 strongly agree scale, with Trump's actions on encouraging the development of fossil fuels over renewable energy. The climate cue was a question regarding agreement with the need to increase the use of renewable sources to address climate change. Random assignment produced approximately equal groups, with the Trump cue, _n_ = `r n_trump`, the climate cue, _n_ = `r n_climate`, and control, _n_ = `r n_control`. The total survey sample was _N_ = `r n_total`).^[A comparison of covariates across the three conditions is presented in the supplemental methods appendix.] 

To measure political beliefs, respondents were asked with which political party they most identify and to place themselves on a 1 to 7 scale of political ideology, where 1 is strongly liberal, 4 is middle of the road, and 7 is strongly conservative. Response were combined so that liberal Democrats are those that identify with the Democratic party and are strongly liberal or liberal, and conservative Republicans are those that are Republicans and strongly conservative or conservative. Other political beliefs, including conservative Democrats, liberal Republicans, and moderate independents are the excluded referents.
