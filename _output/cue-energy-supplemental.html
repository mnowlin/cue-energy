Supplemental Materials: Party or Climate? The Polarization of the US Public’s Energy Preferences

Methods and Robustness Checks

Survey Sample

Despite the use of quotas, the sample from Cloud Research was not perfectly representative of the US population. Specifically, the sample was younger, whiter, more educated, and more aligned with the Democratic party than the US population. To address this, I used the survey and anesrake packages in R to perform iterative raking to compute survey weights that align the sample with the US Census’s 2023 American Community Survey (ACS) on key demographic variables, including age, race, gender, and education. Additionally, I used the Pew Research Center’s National Public Opinion Reference Survey (NPORS) from 2025 to align the sample on political party affiliation.1 The differences between the unweighted and weighted sample are shown in Table 1. The weights were then applied to all analyses in the paper.

Robustness: Results Without Survey Weights

To confirm that the main results are not an artifact of the survey weights, I re-estimate the preferred-mix models via unweighted OLS in place of the weighted svyglm models, using the same specification (party * treatment for each energy source). Table 2 replicates the pairwise-differences table from the main text, and Table 3 replicates the difference-in-differences table. The estimates are substantively unchanged from the weighted analysis, indicating that the reported cue effects are not a product of the raking weights.

Covariate Balance Across Conditions

Respondents were randomly assigned to the Trump cue, climate cue, or control condition. Table 4 compares demographic covariates across the three conditions to confirm that random assignment produced comparable groups. Of the covariates examined, the conditions differ significantly only on age and the share of white respondents; however, political beliefs (liberal Democrats and conservative Republicans) are balanced across conditions.

Robustness: Controlling for Demographic Covariates

Because the conditions differ by chance on age and race (see Table 4), I re-estimate the main difference-in-differences models adding age, sex, race, education, and income as additive controls. Table 5 and Table 6 report the same contrasts as the main-text pairwise-differences and difference-in-differences tables. The estimated treatment effects on the partisan gap are substantively unchanged from the main specification, indicating that the cue effects are not driven by the chance covariate imbalances across conditions.

One contrast crosses the conventional significance threshold once controls are added: for fossil fuels, the Trump-vs-climate shift in the partisan gap is significant at p = 0.050 in the main specification but only at p = 0.063 with controls. The point estimate itself is nearly unchanged (−5.4 vs. −4.98 percentage points, with heavily overlapping confidence intervals), so this reflects the estimate sitting at the edge of the p < 0.05 boundary rather than a substantively different finding.

Preregistered Hypotheses

In addition to the hypotheses present in the paper, I also preregistered other hypotheses that were not included in the paper. These include hypotheses about the effects of political beliefs on support/opposition to the climate change and Trump cues, as well as hypotheses about how the general impacts of the climate change and Trump cues, regardless of political ideology.

CUES Conservative Republicans will be more supportive of Trump’s actions to increase the use of fossil fuels and decrease the use of renewable sources than those with other political beliefs

Liberal Democrats will be more supportive of increasing the use of renewable sources to address climate change than those with other political beliefs

ENERGY MIX Those who receive the Trump cue will support a higher percentage of fossil fuels than those in the climate change or control group

Those who receive the climate cue will support a higher percentage of renewable energy sources (wind, solar, hydropower) than those in the Trump or control group

Preferred Energy Mix by Treatment Condition

Table 7 and Table 8 show the mean preferred percentage allocation for each energy source under each treatment condition, pooled across all respondents (i.e., not split by political beliefs). The p-value in each row tests whether the three condition means differ for that energy source (a design-based F-test for the weighted table, a one-way ANOVA for the unweighted table).
