Supplemental Materials: Party or Climate? The Polarization of the US Public’s Energy Preferences

Methods

Survey Sample

Respondents were recruited from Cloud Research, a survey panel provider that uses a variety of online recruitment methods to build a large and diverse panel of respondents. Cloud Research provides a quota-based sampling approach, where US Census data is used to ensure representation across key demographic groups. The survey was internet based, and administered in QuestionPro. It was fielded from April 10 to April 15, 2026.

Despite the use of quotas, the sample is not perfectly representative of the US population. Specifically, the sample was slightly younger, whiter, more educated, and more aligned with the Democratic party than the US population. To address this, I used the survey and anesrake packages in R to perform iterative raking to compute survey weights that align the sample with the US Census’s 2023 American Community Survey (ACS) on key demographic variables, including age, race, gender, and education. Additionally, I used the Pew Research Center’s National Public Opinion Reference Survey (NPORS) from 2025 to align the sample on political party affiliation.1 The differences between the unweighted and weighted sample are shown in Table 1. The weights were then applied to all analyses in the paper.

Covariate Balance Across Conditions

Respondents were randomly assigned to the Trump cue, climate cue, or control condition. Table 2 compares covariates across the three conditions to confirm that random assignment produced comparable groups. Of the covariates examined, the conditions differ significantly only on age and the share of white respondents; the pre-treatment political-beliefs moderators (liberal Democrats and conservative Republicans) are balanced across conditions.

Robustness: Controlling for Demographic Covariates

Because the conditions differ by chance on age and race (see Table 2), I re-estimate the main difference-in-differences models adding age, sex, race, education, and income as additive controls. Table 3 reports the same contrasts as the main-text difference-in-differences table. The estimated treatment effects on the partisan gap are substantively unchanged from the main specification, indicating that the cue effects are not driven by the chance covariate imbalances across conditions.

Pairwise Differences in the Preferred Energy Mix

Table 4 reports design-based tests of the differences in the predicted preferred energy mix shown in the main text. The partisan-gap rows test the difference between liberal Democrats and conservative Republicans within each condition; the within-party rows test whether each party’s preferred share shifts across conditions. The partisan gaps are significant for every source and condition, whereas the within-party cue effects are concentrated among conservative Republicans in the Trump condition.

Survey Question Wording

Trump cue: As you may have heard, the Trump administration has taken steps to increase the production of fossil fuels, particularly coal, and reduce the use of renewable energy like solar and wind for electricity generation.

Using a scale of 1 to 5, where 1 means strongly oppose and 5 means strongly support, how do you feel about the Trump administration’s goal of increasing the use of fossil fuels and decreasing the use of renewable sources in generating electricity?

Climate cue: Scientists agree that the use of fossil fuels has contributed to climate change, and to address climate change, we need to increase the use of renewable sources such as wind, solar, and hydropower.

Using a scale of 1 to 5, where 1 means strongly disagree and 5 means strongly agree, how do you feel about increasing the use of renewable sources in generating electricity as a way to address climate change?

Thinking about the overall supply of electricity. What percentage of the total U.S. electricity supply over the next 20 years would you like to see come from each of the 5 primary sources?

Additional Hypotheses

In addition to the hypotheses present in the paper, I also preregistered other hypotheses that were not included in the paper. These include hypotheses about the effects of political beliefs on support/opposition to the climate change and Trump cues, as well as hypotheses about how the general impacts of the climate change and Trump cues, regardless of political ideology.

CUES Conservative Republicans will be more supportive of Trump’s actions to increase the use of fossil fuels and decrease the use of renewable sources than those with other political beliefs

Liberal Democrats will be more supportive of increasing the use of renewable sources to address climate change than those with other political beliefs

ENERGY MIX Those who receive the Trump cue will support a higher percentage of fossil fuels than those in the climate change or control group

Those who receive the climate cue will support a higher percentage of renewable energy sources (wind, solar, hydropower) than those in the Trump or control group
